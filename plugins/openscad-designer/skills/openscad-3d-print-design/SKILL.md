# OpenSCAD 3D-Print Design

Two things make a model actually good for 3D printing, not just geometrically valid: it has to **sit and stick** on the print bed, and it has to be **printable without supports** wherever reasonably possible. Both are easy to get wrong in ways that look completely fine in a single render and only show up when you check the model from angles you weren't already looking at, or do the arithmetic on where a feature actually lands. This skill is about building that checking habit in, not just producing *a* .scad file.

The loop is: **design with print constraints in mind → validate → render from every angle → look hard for the bugs that hide from a single view → fix → repeat → export.** Don't skip straight to export after the first render that "looks right" — the cheapest render is the one that catches a bug before it's baked into 20 minutes of failed printing.

## Design principles

These come from real failure modes, not hypotheticals — each one below was something that looked correct until checked properly.

**Every part has to actually touch, not just look close.** This is the single most common way these models come out broken, so it gets called out before anything else: a part positioned or rotated to sit right next to another (an arm against a body, an ear against a head, a wheel against an axle) can end up a fraction of a millimeter short of actually overlapping it. `union()` doesn't merge two solids that don't intersect — it just keeps them as two separate shells in the same file. The geometry still validates cleanly, still exports cleanly, and can look completely fused in a render if the gap is small or the camera angle hides it. On the printer, a piece like that either prints as a loose chunk sitting unattached next to the model, or — if it doesn't reach the bed — as unsupported plastic extruded into thin air, which fails outright. Manifold checks and "no errors" from validate/export **do not catch this**, because each disconnected shell is individually perfectly manifold; see the mandatory connectivity check in the workflow below, which is the only reliable way to catch it. When positioning any part against another, build in deliberate overlap (a millimeter or two into the neighboring solid) rather than trying to land exactly on the surface — a slight overlap union()s away invisibly, while landing short is a silent, total failure.

**Walls and shells need real thickness, not just a non-zero one.** A shell wall thinner than about two nozzle-widths (roughly 0.8mm on a common 0.4mm nozzle, so design toward 1.2-2mm as a comfortable general default unless there's a specific reason to go thinner) doesn't get two full perimeter lines — the slicer either prints a single weak, poorly-fused line, or drops the wall from the print entirely. This is easy to introduce by accident: a `difference()` where the inner cavity dimensions were sized without explicitly subtracting a wall-thickness variable, an offset that's slightly too generous, or a parameter typo that shrinks a gap intended to be a solid wall. It's also easy to miss by looking: a 0.3mm wall on a 100mm model is a handful of pixels in a render and looks fine at a glance, and OpenSCAD's manifold check doesn't measure thickness at all — a paper-thin shell is still perfectly manifold. Give every shell/wall an explicit named thickness variable in the source (rather than computing it implicitly from two independently-chosen outer/inner dimensions) so it's a single number to reason about and adjust, and run the mandatory thickness check in the workflow below rather than trusting a render to catch it.

**A flat base needs real contact area, not a tangent point.** If you rest a sphere (or any curved solid) on the bed by placing its center at exactly `z = radius` and clipping at `z = 0`, the "flat" bottom is a single mathematical point — zero contact area, and the print will not adhere or will wobble. Instead, push the center down so a genuine cap gets sliced off: if the sphere center sits at `z = radius - cut_depth`, the resulting flat circle has radius `sqrt(radius^2 - (radius - cut_depth)^2)`. Pick a `cut_depth` that gives a comfortably large disc (tens of percent of the part's own radius, not a sliver). This applies to any rounded shape, not just spheres — always ask "if I flatten this, is the flat face actually going to have area, or am I just touching the plane?"

**Keep unsupported overhangs within ~45° of vertical.** FDM printers can bridge a slope up to about 45° from vertical without drooping; beyond that, unsupported material sags or fails outright. A cantilevered arm, branch, or spike sticking out sideways is the classic trap — a 60°-from-vertical arm looks fine in a still render but is a near-horizontal cantilever in reality. When adding a protruding feature, ask what angle it makes with the vertical build axis, not with the surface it's attached to. This is genuinely hard to judge by eye across a whole model — a steep underside hides from most camera angles — so it gets the same treatment as the other invisible failure modes: a dedicated script, `scripts/check_overhangs.py`, that measures every downward-facing surface against the build axis in the model's actual export orientation and reports which ones need support (see the mandatory checks in the workflow below).

**Small horizontal protrusions (like a nose) can get away with more, if they're short and tapered.** A cone-shaped tip under roughly 10-15mm long, tapering to a point, will usually bridge fine even close to horizontal — slicers handle short bridges and self-tapering shapes well. This is different from a long arm or beam, which will sag at the same angle. Use judgment on scale, not just angle.

**Prefer dimples over bumps for small surface details.** Eyes, buttons, engraved details, logos — if they can be shallow indentations (subtracted spheres/shapes) instead of raised bumps, do that. A dimple needs zero support and prints cleanly; a small bump on a curved surface can itself be a tiny overhang. This one is close to a free win, so default to it.

**Large flat faces are the best print orientation you can ask for — use them.** A hat brim, a base plate, a flange: if a part of the design is naturally flat, make sure it actually IS flat (a true plane, not an approximation) and orient the model so that face can sit on the bed or print as a horizontal top surface. This also tends to make the model look cleaner, not just print better.

**Moderate overlap between unioned rounded parts, don't over-fuse them.** Stacking spheres or rounded blobs with heavy overlap avoids concave overhang pockets at the joints, but too much overlap makes distinct parts blur into one fat mass and often isn't necessary for printability anyway — a shallow overlap (just enough that there's no razor-thin seam) is usually both structurally fine to print and visually distinct. If a joint needs to look "distinct but connected," bias toward less overlap than your first instinct.

## Deciding what should print as one piece vs. separate, assembled parts

Defaulting to "the whole model is one fused object" is itself a print-optimization miss for a lot of designs, not a safe neutral choice. Some parts have no choice but to be separate — anything that has to move relative to the rest after assembly (a wheel that spins, a hinge, a lid that opens) physically cannot be fused to its neighbor, full stop. Other parts don't strictly have to be separate, but print far better that way: a part whose best orientation on the bed conflicts with the rest of the model, or a part that would need heavy support if welded onto the main body but prints flat and clean entirely on its own, is usually a better candidate for a separate piece with an attachment interface than for forcing into one file. A car is the clearest example — wheels want to print flat on a face with zero support, but fused onto a car body they'd be buried in the middle of the model, printing on their curved edge with support material packed into the wheel well.

When a design has distinct functional sub-parts — anything that rotates, detaches, opens, or is a natural candidate for its own good print orientation — ask explicitly whether it should be its own part before writing the geometry, not as an afterthought once the single-file version already has an overhang problem.

**This is a different decision from the file-splitting below and from the connectivity check above — keep them straight.** `.scad` source can be split across many files while still describing one physical printed object (a car body assembled from `parts/chassis.scad` and `parts/roof.scad` that all `union()` together into a single export is still one piece). What this section is about is the *physical* object: some parts are meant to be genuinely separate prints, assembled or left free to move after printing, never unioned in OpenSCAD at all. When that's the intent, export each part as its own STL. A wheel and a car body that are supposed to print separately should never show up as two disconnected shells inside one combined export — that's not "detachable parts," that's the exact floating-geometry bug the connectivity check exists to catch. The dividing line is clean: intentionally separate parts get their own exports (and each export should itself pass the connectivity check as a single piece); anything that ends up disconnected *within* one export is a bug to fix, not a design choice.

**Design the joint's fit to match whether the parts are meant to move.** These want opposite tolerances, and it's an easy thing to get backwards:
- **Static attachment** (should stay put once assembled — a handle, a decorative add-on, a lid meant to be glued): use a slight interference fit, a peg a few tenths of a millimeter *larger* than its socket, so the pieces press together firmly by hand or with a dab of glue.
- **Moving/functional attachment** (a wheel on an axle, a hinge pin, anything meant to rotate or slide after assembly): use a clearance fit, a peg a few tenths of a millimeter *smaller* than its socket, so it can move freely once printed. FDM tolerances vary by printer, but roughly 0.2-0.4mm of clearance per side is a reasonable starting point to offer the user, who can fine-tune it for their own printer.
- A small retaining lip or flare right at the tip of an axle (slightly larger than the hub bore) keeps a wheel captive without glue while still letting it spin freely.

**Choosing the right tolerance number on paper doesn't guarantee the geometry actually has it — verify with `scripts/check_interference.py`.** It's easy for the two mating dimensions to end up wrong in practice even when the intended clearance/interference is correctly reasoned about: a variable used on one side of the joint but not the other, a diameter vs. radius mix-up, two parts positioned a hair off-axis so they collide on one side while gapping on the other, or — just as easy to miss — an unrelated bystander feature (a cosmetic boss, a fillet, a label) that happens to protrude into the joint area even though the peg/socket dimensions themselves are correct. None of this shows up in the assembly preview render, because previewing usually means `union()`-ing everything together, which silently absorbs any amount of overlap into one smooth shape — a collision looks exactly as clean in a render as a perfect fit. To check for real: build a small throwaway file that computes `intersection() { partA(); partB(); }` with both parts at their true assembled position (not their individual print orientation), and export it. What counts as a pass depends on which kind of joint it is:
- **Clearance/moving joint**: the export should fail with "Current top level object is empty." That failure *is* the pass — it means zero overlap. If it exports successfully at all, something is colliding (correctly-fitted peg/axle dimensions or not) and needs to be tracked down before trusting the joint will move freely.
- **Static/interference joint**: some overlap is intentional, so a successful export is expected. Run `python3 <skill-dir>/scripts/check_interference.py path/to/intersection.stl` on it to confirm the overlap is the right size, not just present — it reports a "characteristic thickness" for the interference (robust to shape, so it won't be fooled by a round peg's overlap forming a thin ring that spans the peg's full diameter) and flags anything beyond a few tenths of a millimeter as a real collision that will jam on assembly rather than a usable press fit. It splits the interference solid into separate connected pieces first and measures each on its own, rather than one average over the whole file — a design with several joints checked in one pass, or a joint where a big legitimate near-zero-clearance area sits alongside one small real jam, would otherwise let a single global average dilute the bad spot below the threshold; per-piece measurement (with a location for each) catches it instead.

Not every model benefits from this — a single-orientation object with no moving or awkwardly-oriented parts (a nameplate, a simple hook) has no reason to be anything but one piece, and splitting it would just be unnecessary assembly for the user. Reach for multi-part design when there's a real functional or printability reason, and when it changes the deliverable, say so plainly — "the wheels print separately from the body and press-fit onto the axles" tells the user how many files to expect and how to put it together, which they need to know before they start printing.

## Structuring complex models across multiple files

A single `.scad` file is fine for something snowman-sized, but once a model has several distinct physical parts, or reusable mechanical elements (fasteners, gears, enclosures) that show up more than once, split it up rather than letting one file balloon into an unreadable wall of modules. A layout that works well:

```
project/
├── main.scad
├── lib/
│   ├── fasteners.scad     // screws, nuts, standoffs
│   ├── enclosures.scad    // box shells, lids, snap-fits
│   └── gears.scad         // involute gears, etc.
└── parts/
    ├── bracket.scad
    └── housing.scad
```

`main.scad` is the top-level assembly: it pulls in `lib/` and `parts/`, positions everything, and is the file you actually validate/render/export. `lib/` holds reusable, project-specific building blocks — modules meant to be called from multiple places. `parts/` holds the distinct physical pieces of the design, each generally something that could stand on its own.

This split is a judgment call, not a default to reach for on every model. It earns its keep once there's real reuse — shared fastener logic across several parts, or enough physical pieces that one file gets hard to navigate. For something with just two or three simple parts and no shared logic, a flat file is genuinely less overhead, and a multi-file layout there is more ceremony than benefit — don't split just because a model happens to have more than one part.

Use `use <file.scad>` to pull in modules/functions from another file without re-running its top-level statements — this is the right default for library and part files, since it avoids variable clashes and accidentally rendering another file's top-level geometry into yours. Reach for `include <file.scad>` only when you deliberately need that file's global variables or top-level side effects too.

When iterating on one part of a multi-file model, it's often faster to validate/render that part's own file standalone (with a temporary top-level call to just that module) before re-checking it in the full assembly — a fast, isolated feedback loop on the piece you're actually changing beats re-rendering the whole assembly every time. If the part depends on BOSL2, remember each standalone file needs its own `include <BOSL2/std.scad>` (see below) — it doesn't inherit through a `use` chain from a lib file.

**Don't reinvent mechanical primitives that a library already solved well.** Screw threads, nut traps, snap-fit joints, involute gears, rounded/chamfered boxes — these are fiddly to get both geometrically correct and print-friendly from scratch, and mature libraries already encode the tolerances and support-free-printing lessons that took their authors many iterations to get right (the same kind of lessons this skill's design-principles section is built from). [BOSL2](https://github.com/BelfrySCAD/BOSL2) is the standard choice for hardware-adjacent geometry — `screw()`, `nut()`, `gear()`, rounded-box primitives, attachable positioning — but treat it as an environment dependency to verify, not something you can assume is installed:

- **Check it resolves before building real geometry on it, not after.** `include <BOSL2/std.scad>` when the library isn't on OpenSCAD's include path doesn't hard-fail — it prints a "can't find include file" warning and quietly continues, so anything built on it renders as a bare/incomplete shape rather than erroring out, and `validate-model` alone won't reliably catch this either. Rather than writing a bunch of BOSL2-dependent design and only discovering it's broken once you render it, spend one cheap render up front: a throwaway file containing just `include <BOSL2/std.scad>; cube(1); #cuboid(2);` (or any trivial BOSL2 call) confirms the library actually resolves before you invest in anything real. If it's missing, the usual fix is cloning BOSL2 into OpenSCAD's default library directory (e.g. `~/Documents/OpenSCAD/libraries/BOSL2` on macOS) rather than falling back to hand-rolled threads — do this once per environment, not once per model.
- **`include <BOSL2/std.scad>` doesn't pull in every submodule.** Some functionality lives in separate files under the same tree — for example `screw()`/`screw_hole()` are in `BOSL2/screws.scad`, not `std.scad`. If a BOSL2 call comes back as an unknown module, check whether it needs its own explicit `include` for a more specific file within the library.
- **Every top-level file needs its own `include <BOSL2/std.scad>`**, even ones only reached via `use` from another file — BOSL2's attachable/tag system relies on special `$`-prefixed variables being set up directly in the file OpenSCAD is rendering, and that setup doesn't propagate transitively through a `use` chain the way plain modules do.
- **Don't assume every parameter combination in a BOSL2 (or any library) call is valid** — some option combinations legitimately raise assertion errors deep inside the library (e.g. certain `screw_hole()` tolerance/thread combinations). If a call throws from inside library code rather than your own, check the library's own examples for the working pattern rather than guessing at nearby parameter tweaks.

Reach for BOSL2 whenever a design calls for hardware-adjacent geometry rather than hand-building threads or gear teeth from primitives — it's still the right call, just budget a bit of setup verification rather than assuming the include line alone will work.

## OpenSCAD gotchas worth knowing up front

These are easy to get subtly wrong and won't throw an error — the model will validate and render, just not do what you meant.

- **A part based at a solid's center, not its surface, needs its own length to cover the radius before it protrudes at all.** If you `translate()` a cylinder to a sphere's center and then extrude it outward, the cylinder's length must exceed the sphere's radius before any of it appears outside — otherwise the whole thing is invisibly buried. Always compute: does this feature's length/reach exceed the distance from its anchor point to the surface it needs to poke through?
- **`rotate([θ,0,0])` direction is easy to get backwards.** Applied to a cylinder that defaults to pointing along +Z, `rotate([θ,0,0])` sends the tip to `y' = -sin(θ)·h, z' = cos(θ)·h`. It's very easy to flip the sign and have a nose, arm, or spike pointing directly away from where it should be, invisible from the "obvious" viewing angle and only caught by checking from the side or back. When something you added doesn't show up in a render, check whether it's pointing the wrong way before assuming it's missing.
- **A subtracted detail can land inside the wrong solid when parts overlap.** If a dimple's anchor point is close to the surface of the part you intended, but ALSO happens to be inside a neighboring overlapping part (e.g., a "head" dimple that's actually still inside the "body" sphere in the overlap/neck region), the visible surface there belongs to the other part, and your dimple gets buried. Check the point's distance to every nearby part's center, not just the one you were aiming at.
- **The loom preview camera format is `eye_x,y,z,center_x,y,z`, and `viewAll: true` (the default) auto-fits the whole object regardless of your requested distance** — and setting `viewAll: false` is not a reliable way to force a genuine close-up either. If you need to actually see a small feature clearly, bump the output resolution (`width`/`height`) on a full-object shot rather than fighting the camera for a zoomed crop.

## Workflow

1. **Before writing geometry, decide what should print as one piece vs. separate assembled parts** (see above) — this shapes the design from the start rather than being a fix bolted on after a single-file version turns out to need heavy supports. Then write the `.scad` file(s) with the other design principles in mind too — it's much cheaper to design in a flat base and shallow overhangs than to retrofit them after the geometry is built. Use a reasonable `$fn` (32-64) for smooth curves without making renders slow. If the model has multiple distinct parts or reusable mechanical elements, split the source into files per the project-structure section below rather than defaulting to one flat file; and before hand-building anything hardware-adjacent (threads, gears, snap-fits), check whether BOSL2 already has it.
2. **`mcp__loom__validate-model`** for a fast syntax/geometry sanity check before spending time on renders.
3. **`mcp__loom__render-preview` from enough angles to actually see the whole thing**: front, back, left, right, top, bottom, and at least one 3/4 perspective. Compute real coordinates for camera targets (e.g., the actual z-height of a face) rather than eyeballing — it's the difference between a render that shows the feature you're checking and one that doesn't.
4. **Look specifically for the failure modes above**: does the base genuinely read as flat from below (not just a silhouette that looks round from any angle)? Do all the features you added actually show up, on the correct side? Are there any protrusions that look closer to horizontal than 45° from vertical? Any part that visually looks like it's ballooned into its neighbor when it shouldn't have?
5. **Fix, re-validate, re-render** — don't just fix one thing and export; re-check the angles that would reveal a regression.
6. **`mcp__loom__export-model`** once the model is clean. If step 1's decision was to keep everything as one printed piece, export the single combined STL as usual. If any parts were deliberately designed to print separately (wheels, lids, anything meant to move or print in a different orientation), export **each intended piece as its own STL** — a temporary top-level file per part (or per part's own file, per the structuring section) that renders only that part, so what gets exported matches what's meant to be printed.
7. **Run `scripts/check_connectivity.py`, `scripts/check_wall_thickness.py`, and `scripts/check_overhangs.py` on every exported STL, every time, no exceptions — and `scripts/check_interference.py` on every mating joint between separately-printed parts.** None of these are optional visual QA — they're the checks in this workflow that a render literally cannot substitute for, because all these failure modes are invisible to OpenSCAD's own manifold check and easy to miss by eye:
   - `python3 <skill-dir>/scripts/check_connectivity.py path/to/model.stl` reports how many disconnected pieces exist and roughly where — a part a fraction of a millimeter short of touching its neighbor is manifold-clean but silently a second shell. Each exported file should report exactly one piece (whether that's the whole fused model, or one part of an intentionally multi-part design).
   - `python3 <skill-dir>/scripts/check_wall_thickness.py path/to/model.stl` reports the thinnest wall found against a threshold (defaults to 1.2mm; pass `--nozzle 0.4` or similar to derive it from the target printer's nozzle instead) — a wall that's technically non-zero but too thin to print as two solid perimeters is also manifold-clean and easy to miss at render scale.
   - `python3 <skill-dir>/scripts/check_overhangs.py path/to/model.stl` reports every downward-facing surface that leans more than ~45° from vertical (pass `--threshold` to tune, or `--up` if the part doesn't print Z-up) and would need support — grouped into regions with a location, area, and horizontal span for each. This one is a **review signal, not a hard pass/fail** like the two above: the STL must be in its intended print orientation for the result to mean anything (rotate the part flat in the `.scad` before exporting the file you check), and a non-empty result isn't automatically a bug — some parts genuinely can't avoid all support. The goal is "minimal support wherever reasonably possible," so treat each flagged region as a prompt to try reorienting the part, adding a chamfer/fillet, or splitting it into separately-printed pieces first; accept support only when it's the deliberate, unavoidable choice. The script flags a short flat span as a possible bridge (often prints clean without support) so you don't chase overhangs that aren't really problems.
   - When the design has any parts meant to print separately and mate afterward (see the section above), also export `intersection() { partA(); partB(); }` at their true assembled position for each joint, and run `python3 <skill-dir>/scripts/check_interference.py path/to/intersection.stl` on it — an empty export means no overlap (fine for a clearance fit), otherwise it measures how deep the collision runs and flags anything beyond a normal interference-fit tolerance, which the assembly-preview render (usually a `union()`) can't reveal at all.

   If a script flags a problem, that tells you which part and roughly where — fix it in the `.scad` source (add overlap for a disconnected piece; thicken the relevant wall variable for a thin one; reorient, chamfer, or split to shed an overhang; adjust the peg/socket dimensions for a jammed joint), re-export, and re-run the relevant checks until they're clean (or, for overhangs, until what remains is a deliberate, minimal, reported-to-the-user choice). For a multi-part design, run connectivity, thickness, and overhangs on **each** part's own STL — each part prints in its own orientation, so each needs its own overhang pass.
8. **Report back concisely**: confirm each exported model is manifold, single-piece, and passes the thickness check (not just "Status: NoError" from validate/export — see why above), and that any mating joints pass the interference check. State the support picture plainly per part: either it prints support-free in the given orientation, or name where support is needed and why it was unavoidable (e.g. "the underside of the overhanging arm needs support; reorienting would bury the flat face it's designed to show"). If the design has multiple printed parts, say so explicitly and describe how they go together (e.g. "the wheels print separately and press-fit onto the axles — 0.3mm clearance so they still spin"). Call out anything else the user should know for slicing — e.g. "the nose is short enough to bridge without support, but if you scale this up, it'll need one."

Don't treat "it rendered without errors" as "it's done" — a model can be perfectly manifold and still have an invisible feature, a backwards rotation, a single-point base, geometry that's silently missing because an `include`/`use` failed to resolve, a part that never actually attached to the rest of the model, a wall too thin to print reliably, or a steep overhang that will droop into a failed print. Renders catch most of those if you look at the right angle; the connectivity, thickness, and overhang scripts are what catch the last three, which renders systematically miss. Budget the turns for all of it.
